💨
Dissolved Oxygen (DO)
Most critical parameter · #1 cause of mass mortality
Target ≥ 5 mg/L day · ≥ 4 mg/L dawn
⬇ Low DO — Sub-threshold (<4 mg/L)
Shrimp crowd surface at 2–5 AM; lethargic; reduced feeding; paddlewheel creates foam but water murky
  • Emergency: Run all aerators NOW — do not delayDawn (2–6 AM) is peak danger window. DO crashes first at pond bottom where shrimp live.
  • Water exchange — pump fresh, well-oxygenated water 15–25%Avoid pulling in low-DO canal water. Use pre-treated reservoir only.
  • Reduce / stop feeding immediately Until DO ≥ 5
  • CaO (quicklime) — aerobic microbe suppression 5–10 kg/acre
  • Zeolite — bottom ammonia absorption 10–15 kg/acre
⬆ Supersaturation (>12 mg/L, peak afternoon)
Gas bubble disease; emboli in haemolymph; shrimp float with gas under carapace; peak after dense algae bloom + sunny day
  • Reduce algae density — partial water exchange 10–15%
  • Reduce or cut feeding
  • Shade cloth on extreme sunny days
⚠ Dense Bloom RiskAlgae bloom produces O₂ during day (DO 12+) then consumes O₂ at night — classic crash pattern. Dawn monitoring non-negotiable. 72–89% of nutrient input ends up in sediment fuelling this cycle.
⚖️
pH — Pond Acid-Base Balance
Diurnal swing ≤ 0.5 optimal · Controls NH₃ toxicity
Target 7.5–8.5 (AM 7.8 · PM 8.3)
⬇ Low pH (<7.5)
H₂S toxicity increases; shrimp stress; shell softening; low plankton productivity; acidic soil leaching metals
  • Agricultural lime Ca(OH)₂ 100–150 kg/acreApply at dusk. Recheck pH next morning. Repeat in 2–3 days if needed.
  • Dolomite (CaMg(CO₃)₂) 100–200 kg/haSlower but longer buffering effect. Best for routine maintenance.
⬆ High pH (>9.0) — Algae bloom photosynthesis
NH₃ fraction jumps sharply — same TAN becomes 10× more toxic at pH 9 vs pH 8; brown/red algae dominance; shrimp irritable
  • Alum (Al₂(SO₄)₃) — if high alkalinity + low Ca 5–10 kg/acre
  • Gypsum (CaSO₄) — if high alkalinity + low Ca hardness 50 kg/acre
  • Reduce algae density — partial water exchange 15–20%
  • Stop fertilisation / reduce feeding immediately
↕ Wide Diurnal Swing (>0.5 units)
  • Dolomite to increase buffering capacity 100–200 kg/ha
  • Partial water exchange to dilute dense plankton
  • Probiotic application — stabilises plankton community
☠️
Ammonia (TAN / NH₃) Toxicity
pH and temperature determine toxic fraction
Target TAN ≤ 0.1 mg/L · NH₃ < 0.01 mg/L
⚠ Elevated TAN (>0.1–0.5 mg/L) — Sublethal
Reduced feeding; gill damage; immune suppression; slow growth; increased Vibrio susceptibility. Higher pH = higher toxic NH₃ fraction (at pH 9, NH₃ is 10× higher than same TAN at pH 8)
  • Stop feeding — cut organic input immediately
  • Water exchange (fastest fix) 15–25%Use clean reservoir water. Canal water may add more ammonia.
  • Zeolite (ammonia adsorption) 15–20 kg/acre
  • Molasses (carbon source for heterotrophs) 5–10 kg/acre dissolvedC:N ratio method — heterotrophic bacteria consume NH₄⁺ when given carbon. Works in 24–48 hrs.
  • Probiotics — Bacillus spp. based 1 kg/acre
⚠ pH Danger MultiplierAt pH 8.5 and 30°C, ~5% of TAN is toxic NH₃. At pH 9.0 same temperature, ~15% is toxic. Any spike in pH during algae bloom drives a sudden ammonia crisis even if TAN appears moderate.
🔶
Nitrite (NO₂⁻) — Brown Blood Disease
Especially dangerous at low salinity (4–10 ppt)
Target < 0.1 mg/L · Cl:NO₂ ≥ 20:1
Why Low-Salinity Ponds Are More Vulnerable
4–8 ppt:Chloride ions that block NO₂ uptake at gills are scarce. Even 0.3 mg/L NO₂ causes chronic stress. Monodon extremely sensitive even at this range.
8–15 ppt:Moderate protection. Keep Cl:NO₂ ratio > 20:1 by supplementing KCl or NaCl if needed.
15–30 ppt:Natural Cl⁻ provides good protection. Standard NO₂ < 0.5 mg/L acceptable.
Nitrite Spike — Common During Biofilter Maturation (DOC 20–40)
Pale gills (brown discolouration); reduced DO uptake (methaemoglobin formation); shrimp appear lethargic; feeding tray shows reduced consumption
  • Emergency salt addition — increases Cl⁻, outcompetes NO₂ 20–50 kg/acre NaClFor inland low-salinity ponds only. Fastest protection against gill uptake.
  • KCl (potassium chloride) — inland low-salinity ponds 10–20 kg/acre
  • Water exchange 20–30%
  • Zeolite — concurrent ammonia spike management 15 kg/acre
🖤
Hydrogen Sulfide (H₂S) — Black Bottom Pond
Silent killer · Rotten egg smell · Bottom-dweller risk
Target 0 — Undetectable
H₂S Buildup — Black Pond Bottom / Rotten Smell
Bottom mud black and malodorous when disturbed; P. monodon most affected (benthic burrower); equilibrium loss at 0.1 ppm; instant death at 4 ppm. Shrimp feed tray empty — first warning sign.
  • Frequent water exchange — prevent accumulation 10–30% dailyMost economical method. Increase frequency as DOC increases and organic load builds.
  • Bottom aeration — agitate sludge layer, re-oxidise Paddle aeration near pond edges. Do NOT aerate aggressively in infected pond — H₂S gas release can spike.
  • CaO (quicklime) on dry areas / pond bottom 200–300 kg/haRaises bottom pH — suppresses sulphate-reducing bacteria. Apply to exposed areas between crops.
  • Zeolite — bottom detoxification 15–20 kg/acre
  • Probiotics (Bacillus) to break organic sludge 1–2 kg/acre
✖ Do NotNever disturb pond bottom violently (drag net) during active H₂S pond — sudden gas release causes immediate mass mortality. Bottom siphoning must be slow and targeted.
🌊
Salinity Stress & Osmotic Shock
Sudden changes > 3 ppt/day are dangerous
Targets vannamei 15–25 · monodon 15–30 ppt
⬇ Low Salinity Drop (Monsoon / Freshwater Influx)
Osmotic stress; shrimp appear bloated (cells swell as water floods in); soft shells; immunity crash; increased Vibrio and disease susceptibility. Most dangerous at < 5 ppt for monodon.
  • Never add large volumes of freshwater at once Acclimate slowly: < 3 ppt change per 24 hours
  • Sea salt / brine addition if available Raise by ≤ 3 ppt/day
  • Mineral supplement — restore ionic balance 5–10 kg/acreReplace K⁺, Mg²⁺ lost when salinity falls. Critical for inland low-salinity ponds.
  • Reduce feeding by 50% during acclimation period
  • β-Glucan in feed — immune support during osmotic stress 0.1–0.2% of feed
⬆ High Salinity (Summer Evaporation — >30 ppt)
Shrimp thin and dehydrated (water leaves body); growth retardation; feeding refusal; moult intervals extend. Vannamei tolerates up to 40 ppt but optimal is 15–25 ppt.
  • Freshwater addition — gradual ≤ 3 ppt reduction/day
  • Increase water depth to reduce evaporation concentrate effect
  • Shade cloth over nursery tanks if possible
🛡️
Alkalinity — pH Buffering Capacity
Critical for moulting · Rises with DOC — must be managed
Target by DOC DOC 1–30: 100–120 · DOC 30–60: 120–150 · DOC 60+: 150–200 ppm
⬇ Low Alkalinity (<80 ppm) — pH Buffering Failure
Wide pH swings (drops at night, spikes in afternoon); moulting failure; soft shell syndrome; shrimp calcium uptake impaired; shells incomplete and fragile
  • Dolomite CaMg(CO₃)₂ — most balanced choice 100–200 kg/ha every 2–3 daysUntil alkalinity reaches target range. Replenishes both Ca and Mg.
  • Agricultural lime Ca(OH)₂ — faster acting 100–150 kg/acre
  • Sodium bicarbonate NaHCO₃ — gentle, no pH spike 5–10 kg/acre in intensive ponds
⬆ Very High Alkalinity (>200 ppm) — Phytoplankton Crash Risk
Excessive alkalinity reduces CO₂ availability; plankton composition shifts; water turns clear suddenly (crash risk); carbonate precipitation turbidity
  • Gypsum (CaSO₄) — lowers alkalinity without pH harm 50 kg/acre
  • Dilution with freshwater or low-alkalinity water 10–15%
🌿
Plankton Bloom, Crash & Harmful Algae
Transparency 30–40 cm · Manage phosphorus & turbidity
Secchi Target 30–40 cm · Green-brown diatom bloom preferred
Dense Bloom (<25 cm Secchi) — Crash Imminent
Secchi < 25 cm; DO supersaturation afternoon; DO crash at night/dawn; shrimp surface at 2–4 AM. 72–89% of phosphorus input drives this cycle.
  • Reduce feed by 50% immediately
  • Water exchange to dilute plankton 15–25%
  • Probiotic (Bacillus) — competes with algae for nutrients 1 kg/acre
  • Do NOT apply algaecide to thick bloom — crash releases ammonia & consumes all DO
Clear Water (>50 cm Secchi) — Plankton Crash
Water suddenly turns clear after dense bloom; DO drops sharply as dead algae decompose; ammonia spike; shrimp stress. Very dangerous 24-hour window.
  • Emergency aeration — all aerators ON
  • Reduce feeding to zero for 24–48 hrs
  • Zeolite — ammonia absorption 15–20 kg/acre
  • Probiotic + molasses — restart bacterial cycle 1 kg + 5 kg/acre
  • Inoculate with good plankton from reservoir within 48 hrs
⚠ Harmful Algae WarningCyanobacteria (blue-green) bloom produces microcystin hepatotoxin. Identify by dense surface scum with musty odour. Do NOT apply CuSO₄ without species ID — killing bloom in situ crashes DO.
🌡️
Temperature Stress Management
Optimal 28–30°C · Affects metabolism, immunity & DO
Optimal 28–30°C · Vannamei range: 15–33°C
⬇ Cold Stress (<24°C) — Winter / Monsoon
Shrimp cluster at pond bottom; feeding stops; metabolism slows; FCR worsens; immune response drops; bacterial infections increase; soft shell syndrome risk
  • Reduce feed by 50% below 24°C — completely stop below 20°C
  • Raise water level to 1.5–1.8 m depth — thermal buffering
  • Vitamin C + immune booster in feed — cold stress support 1–2 g/kg feed
  • Probiotics — maintain gut microbiome during reduced metabolism 0.5 kg/acre fortnightly
  • Harvest early if temperature expected to remain < 22°C for >2 weeks
⬆ Heat Stress (>33°C) — Summer Peak
High metabolic O₂ demand + reduced DO solubility → crisis. Feeding frenzy then sudden lethargy. Shrimp at surface in afternoon. Increased Vibrio growth at high temperature.
  • Increase water exchange using cooler water 10–15%
  • Maximise aeration during 10 AM–4 PM window
  • Increase water depth to ≥ 1.5 m
  • Reduce stocking density if heat persists >5 days (partial harvest)
🧱
Organic Load, Turbid Water & Pond Bottom Management
Sludge accumulation · Black water · High TOM — routine management strategy
Black Water — H₂S + High Organic Load
Rotten smell; pond surface black-brown with oily sheen; dead plankton sedimented
  • MgSO₄ (magnesium sulphate) 5 kg/acre
  • Agricultural lime Ca(OH)₂ 50 kg/acre
  • Groundnut cake (soaked 4 hrs) to flocculate 5–10 kg/acre at 10–11 AM
  • Cut feeding by 50%. Increase aeration immediately.
Turbid Green / Brown — Dense Suspended Solids
Secchi < 25 cm; plankton too dense; DO and pH swing wildly
  • Aquafloc / Poly-aluminium chloride 1 kg/acreApply morning; allow settlement. Check next day.
  • Probiotic cycle — fortnightly to break organic accumulation 1 kg/acre
  • Zeolite — bottom TAN absorption 15 kg/acre
Sludge Build-up / High TOM — DOC 45+ Intensive Ponds
Feed trays show uneaten feed + feces accumulation; pond bottom black zones visible at shallow edges; high H₂S risk
  • Bottom siphoning — targeted, slow Daily after DOC 30 in intensive pondsNever disturb entire bottom at once — gas release risk.
  • Central drain management — keep sludge moving to drain
  • TCCA (trichloroisocyanuric acid) — pond floor disinfection 0.5 kg/acreApply only in areas not occupied by shrimp (edges/feed lines). Do not apply directly to dense population areas.
  • Probiotic (Bacillus licheniformis + B. subtilis) 1–2 kg/acre biweekly

WSSV — White Spot Syndrome Virus
Viral · DNA Virus No Treatment — Prevention Only Mass Mortality in 3–10 Days
$90M
India annual loss
Patil et al. 2025
Agent: White spot syndrome virus (WSSV) — infects 78+ crustacean species. Spread via infected broodstock, wild carriers (crabs, crayfish), contaminated water & equipment. Peak outbreak: rainy season — sudden temperature drop triggers virulence. Prevalence India east coast: 6.48% (down from 25% — improved biosecurity).
Symptoms
Sudden sharp drop in feeding (check trays daily). Shrimp at pond edges, surface swimming erratically. White calcified spots 0.5–2.0 mm under carapace (press carapace, spots visible). Body turns pink-reddish (internal haemorrhage). Loose shells; empty gut. Mortality 30–80% within first 5–7 days of outbreak — entire pond possible within 10 days.
Prevention — Mandatory Biosecurity
  • SPF (Specific Pathogen Free) seed only — PCR tested Hatchery cert.
  • Reservoir + chlorination of all incoming water 30 ppm TCCA · 24 hrs
  • Bird netting over pond — herons are major WSSV vectors
  • Double filter screens on inlet gates (crabs excluded)
  • PCR test sample shrimp at DOC 15, 30, 45MPEDA/NACA approved labs. Early detection = early harvest option.
  • Pond dry + liming between crops CaO 300–400 kg/acre
Management — On Positive Detection
  • No chemical treatment exists — virus has no cure
  • Emergency partial harvest — recover marketable shrimp (>15 g)
  • Stop feeding completely — prevent further viral load amplification
  • β-Glucan in feed — may slow progression if early stage 0.2% of feed
  • Notify MPEDA / State Fisheries — legal requirement
  • Disinfect all equipment + water before discharge TCCA 30 ppm
  • Pond rest ≥ 30 days + CaO treatment before restocking
✖ Critical BiosecurityNever use WSSV-positive pond water for other ponds. Shared nets, feeding equipment, and farm workers can mechanically transfer WSSV. Avoid stocking during endemic outbreak months in your district (check MPEDA NSPAAD surveillance data).
🦠
EHP — Enterocytozoon hepatopenaei
Microsporidian Parasite No Cure — Management Only
$571M
India #1 shrimp loss
Patil et al. 2025 · 78% of total
Agent: Enterocytozoon hepatopenaei — microsporidian infects hepatopancreatic tubule cells. Spores extremely hardy — survive in pond soil for months. Spread via spore-contaminated feed, water, pond soil between crops. Prevalence India east coast: 32.4%; Karnataka: 22.6%. Co-infection with WSSV worsens outcomes.
Symptoms
Key sign: extreme growth variation — same-age shrimp show 30–50% size variation by DOC 35–40. White or pale hepatopancreas (HP shrunk, soft). White/opaque feces in feed trays ("white feces" — though EHP is not the only cause). Slow FCR deterioration. No mass mortality — chronic stunting & poor survival. Shrimp physically look healthy but don't grow.
Prevention (Most Important Intervention)
  • PCR test PL seed before stocking — reject EHP-positive lotsNACA recommends PCR at PL15 stage from hatchery.
  • Aggressive pond preparation between crops — dry, CaO 300 kg/acreEHP spores destroyed by thorough drying + lime. Skip this step = certain re-infection.
  • Pond liner (HDPE) — eliminates spore reservoir in soilMost effective long-term prevention. Investment pays back in 2–3 crops.
  • Chlorination of all incoming water 30 ppm TCCA · 48 hrs
  • Reduce stocking density 60–80 PL/m² max
Management — Active EHP Pond
  • Organic acid (citric acid, fumaric acid) in feed — acidify gut, inhibit spore 3–5 g/kg feed · 10–15 days
  • Probiotics — outcompete EHP in gut environment 1 kg/acre biweekly
  • Regular water exchange — dilute spore load 15–20% daily DOC 40+
  • Reduce stocking — partial harvest by DOC 60 rather than wait for target size
  • Early harvest — if ABW deviation >30%, cut losses early (EHP ponds rarely recover)
⚡
AHPND / Vibriosis — Acute Hepatopancreatic Necrosis
Bacterial · Vibrio parahaemolyticus EMS — Early Mortality <DOC 30
Agent: Vibrio parahaemolyticus (AHPND strains carrying pVA1 plasmid with toxin genes). Also V. harveyi (luminescent vibriosis — glows at night), V. alginolyticus. Prevalence India east coast survey: V. parahaemolyticus 27.7%. Economic loss India vibriosis: USD 40M/yr.
Symptoms
AHPND: Mass mortality within first 20–30 DOC (up to 80% loss). Hepatopancreas pale, white, shrunken, mushy. Empty gut. Shrimp spiral-swim then die. Monodon-baculovirus + vibriosis co-infection common in P. monodon. General Vibriosis: Luminescent shrimp visible at night (V. harveyi); opaque white muscles; necrotic appendages; black gills; empty gut.
Prevention — Water & Pond Microbiome Management
  • TCCA water disinfection 1 ppm in reservoir · 24 hrs before use
  • BKC 80% (benzalkonium chloride) — routine Vibrio control 500 mL/acre · monthly
  • Probiotic (Bacillus spp.) — competitive exclusion of Vibrio 1 kg/acre every 10 days
  • Organic acid — gut acidification inhibits Vibrio colonisation 3–5 g/kg feed
  • Monitor Vibrio TVC in water weekly from DOC 30 Target < 10³ CFU/mL
  • Avoid overfeeding — excess feed ferments to Vibrio-rich sludge
Management — Active Vibriosis
  • pH management — maintain 7.8–8.2 (Vibrio growth optimal at 7.0–7.5) Lime if pH low
  • TCCA pond treatment 0.5 ppm · evening⚠ Turn on all aerators. Check DO next morning. Do not repeat within 5 days.
  • Phage therapy (emerging) — bacteriophages against V. parahaemolyticusNot yet commercially available in India at scale. Research stage.
  • NO antibiotics without confirmed sensitivity test + veterinarian prescription
✖ Antibiotic WarningRoutine antibiotic use drives antibiotic resistance. V. parahaemolyticus MDR (multi-drug resistant) strains common in India. Export rejection risk. Use only with confirmed sensitivity testing and registered fisheries veterinarian.

RMS — Running Mortality Syndrome
Multi-factorial · Environmental + Bacterial Chronic Loss Throughout Culture
Cause: Not a single pathogen — combination of high stocking density, poor seed quality, high Vibrio load, shallow water (<1.2 m), high organic load, and water quality stress. India survey: production loss 1.1 t/ha/crop. Shrimp "run" to edges and die in small numbers daily.
Symptoms
Continuous daily mortality — small numbers (0.01–0.05% of stock per day) dead on edges. Shrimp appear lethargic, gills darkened. No single dramatic event. FCR steadily worsens. Survival at harvest 40–60% in severe cases (expected: 70–80%). No single diagnostic sign — must rule out WSSV, EHP, AHPND by PCR.
Management Strategy
  • Intensive aeration — 24/7 minimum 1 HP/1000 kg biomass
  • Reduce stocking density — partial harvest if >DOC 50 Target < 100/m²
  • Probiotic cycle — 10-day intervals throughout crop 1 kg/acre
  • Molasses — carbon supplement for heterotrophic Vibrio control 5 kg/acre when Vibrio high
  • Water exchange — daily after DOC 40 10–15%
  • Increase water depth to ≥ 1.5 m — thermal & DO buffer
  • Vitamin C + immunostimulants in feed 1–2 g/kg feed continuously
⬜
WFS — White Feces Syndrome / Loose Shell
EHP + Vibrio + Gregarine Gut Health Failure
Causes: White feces = combination of EHP-damaged hepatopancreas, Vibrio gut infection, Gregarine parasites (Nematopsis spp.), poor feed quality, and overfeeding. Loose shell = separate — mineral deficiency (Ca, Mg, alkalinity) + moulting disruption. Often both occur together in stressed ponds.
Symptoms
White / pale stringy feces floating in pond surface & collecting on feed trays. Hepatopancreas pale and shrunken. Loose cuticle (shell separates from body). Empty gut on dissection. Slow growth. Feeding tray shows reduced consumption.
White Feces — Gut Restoration
  • Reduce feeding by 30–50% immediately
  • Probiotic in feed + pond 2 kg/acre pond · 3 g/kg feed
  • Organic acid in feed — citric/fumaric/formic acid 3–5 g/kg feed · 15 days
  • Hepatoprotective (liver tonic) supplement 1–2 kg/ton feed
  • Water exchange — reduce Vibrio & organic load 15–20% daily
Loose Shell — Mineral Restoration
  • Dolomite — Ca + Mg for shell formation 100 kg/acre
  • Ca + Mg mineral supplement in feed 5 g/kg feed
  • Raise alkalinity to 150–180 ppm (moulting support)
  • Vitamin C + K for cuticle integrity 2 g/kg feed
  • Avoid stress during moulting — stable DO, salinity, feeding tray timing

Black Gill Disease & Gill Fouling
Protozoan — Zoothamnium / Epistylis Fusarium spp. Poor DO + High Organic
Cause: Zoothamnium, Epistylis, Vorticella (ciliates) + Fusarium fungus + bacteria colonise gills in poor water quality. High TOM, low DO, high H₂S — triggers colonisation. East coast India survey: gills darkened in 22% of sampled farms with suboptimal water quality.
Symptoms
Gills turn grey, brown or black (in contrast to normal pale pink-white). Shrimp at surface trying to breathe. FCR increases despite adequate feeding. Growth slows. Dark fouling material visible on gill surface under magnification — fluffy or slimy texture. Severe: haemocyte infiltration, gill necrosis.
Management
  • Emergency aeration — all units ON Immediate
  • Water exchange — dilute organic load 20–25%
  • BKC 80% — disinfection + protozoan control 500 mL/acre
  • TCCA water disinfection 0.5 ppm · evening
  • Zeolite — bottom TAN + H₂S management 15 kg/acre
  • Dolomite — raise pH, suppress ciliate growth 100 kg/acre
  • Formalin bath (hatchery/nursery only) 25–50 ppm · 1 hr⚠ NOT for open pond use at this concentration. Degrades gill tissue at excess dose.
PreventionRoutine fortnightly BKC + probiotic alternation significantly reduces black gill incidence. Maintain DO > 5 mg/L — ciliates are obligate aerobe commensals that explosively colonise when host immunity drops from low DO stress.
⚪
White Gut, White Muscle & Muscle Cramp
Mineral Deficiency Osmotic / Temperature Stress
Causes: White gut = gut cleared of microbiome (starvation, Vibrio, EHP). White muscle = magnesium & zinc deficiency + temperature stress (common summer shallow ponds). Muscle cramp (cramped tail, bent body) = osmotic shock, low Mg²⁺, low K⁺, abrupt salinity change, or bacterial muscle necrosis.
Symptoms
White Gut: midgut appears white/empty on dissection; poor feeding response. White Muscle: opaque white patches on abdominal muscles (back); shrimp look partially bleached; mortality in severe cases. Muscle Cramp: shrimp body bent/curled at tail; cannot swim properly; sink to bottom; cramped tail also from net stress during sampling.
White Gut — Gut Restoration
  • Probiotic in feed — Lactobacillus + Bacillus blend 3–5 g/kg feed
  • Prebiotic (MOS/FOS) in feed 2 g/kg feed
  • Organic acid — acidify gut, restore pH 3 g/kg feed
White Muscle / Cramp — Mineral Correction
  • Mg supplement (magnesium sulphate) in feed 3–5 g/kg feed · 10 days
  • Zinc + manganese mineral supplement 1–2 g/kg feed
  • Vitamin E + C — antioxidant muscle support 2 g/kg feed
  • Raise salinity slowly if < 8 ppt — correct osmotic deficit ≤ 3 ppt/day
  • Reduce water temperature — deep water, shade net over nursery
  • K⁺ supplementation (inland low-salinity ponds) KCl 10 kg/acre

Moulting Failure — Soft Shell & Incomplete Moult
Ca / Alkalinity / Mg Deficiency pH Swing + Stress
Cause: Moulting (ecdysis) requires calcium from water + feed + alkalinity buffering. At < 80 ppm alkalinity, Ca uptake fails. Low Mg²⁺ impairs neuromuscular coordination during moult. Abrupt pH, salinity, or temperature change during moult cycle can cause fatal incomplete moult ("stuck in moult"). Cannibalism of moulting shrimp escalates when crowded.
Symptoms
Soft, flexible shell that does not harden within 6–12 hrs post-moult. Empty shells found with shrimp still partially inside (fatal moult). Increased mortality at dawn — post-moult shrimp most vulnerable. Cannibalism marks on soft-shelled shrimp. Reduced weight gain despite adequate feeding — energy spent on repeated failed moults.
Prevention — Moulting Support Protocol
  • Maintain alkalinity at DOC-appropriate levels at all times 120–180 ppm CaCO₃This is the single most important moulting support measure.
  • Dolomite maintenance dose — fortnightly 100 kg/ha
  • Ca + Mg + P mineral supplement in feed 5 g/kg feed
  • Vitamin C — chitin synthesis support 2 g/kg feed
  • Stable salinity — avoid changes > 2 ppt during moult window
  • Reduce stocking density — cannibalism control
  • Night aeration — post-moult shrimp need highest DO
Tip — Moult SynchronyShrimp in a pond moult on similar lunar cycles. Expect elevated vulnerability around new and full moon phases. Increase aeration and do NOT apply chemicals for 48 hrs around these windows.
